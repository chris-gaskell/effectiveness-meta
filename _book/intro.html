The Effectiveness of Psychotherapy Outcomes in Routine Settings

Introduction

Psychotherapy has consistently been demonstrated as efficacious in the treatment of a variety of mental health disorders. A substantial proportion of the support for these claims comes from meta-analytic syntheses of randomised controlled trials ([RCT], e.g. Wampold et al., 1997). Although methodologically robust and high in internal validity, RCTs have been criticised for failing to represent clients that present to routine services. That is, there is a trade-off in generating internal validity at the expense of external validity (Bower & Gilbody, 2010) due to various control parameters (e.g. excluding people showing suicidality, co-morbidity) which are not a feature of routine clinical practice.

An alternative, form of evidence is effectiveness research which assesses treatment findings within ‘real-world’ conditions. Effectiveness research can contribute towards establishing evidence at both the preliminary stage (e.g. novel treatment trialed within routine practice to generate support for an RCT) and the transferability stage (establishing whether findings from RCTs generalise to routine practice). In reality, these three phases (including the RCT stage) represent a complimentary research cycle which has been termed as the ‘hour-glass model’ of outcomes research (Salkovskis reference). This cycle is necessary for verifying treatment effectiveness of interventions applied within the health settings that they were purposefully designed for.

Since the mid 1990s there has been a sharp rise in the amount of practice-based evidence reported within peer-reviewed journals. Prior meta-analytic reviews of such effectiveness research have, for the most part, been supportive (e.g. Cahill, Barkham, & Stiles, 2010; Hans & Hiller, 2013; Shaddish, Matt, Navarro, & Phillips, 2000; Stewart & Chambless, 2009). More recently, the distinction between ‘effectiveness’ and ‘efficacy’ research has become increasingly blurred. One of the principal reasons for this is the rise of evidence-based practice within routine practice. This has led to a range of ‘best practices’ (e.g. outcome measures, manual informed treatments, competency measures), previously restricted largely to RCTs, being far more widely implemented. This has left any efficacy/effectiveness distinction point as being largely arbitrary.

Reviews of effectiveness studies to date have followed one of two approaches. The first is to rate studies on how clinically representative they are and then to see if this correlates with clinical outcomes (e.g. Hans & Hiller, 2013; Shaddish, Matt, Navarro, & Phillips, 2000; Stewart & Chambless, 2009). The main issue with this approach is, again, that many of the criteria used for being *clinically unrepresentative() (e.g. manualised treatment, includes clinicians in training,use of competency check) are, at least by today’s standards, not unknown to routine services. A second approach is to agree upon an acceptable set of threshold criteria as to what constitutes representative practice. This yard-stick approach falls criticism to the arbitrary nature of what constitutes being ‘routine’ or ‘naturalistic’. Using this approach Cahill, Barkham, and Stiles (2010) with criteria for ‘highly representative’ studes were able to identify and review 31 studies. The review supported the effectiveness of psychotherapy in routine settings and found that more methodologically rigorous studies produced greater effect-sizes.

Although prior reviews provide more than sufficient evidence for the effectiveness of psychotherapy in routine settings, there has far been less meta-analytic consideration of the factors that may under-pin, or moderate this relationship. Establishing which factors may influence/moderate the effectiveness of psychotherapy has utility for services and policy makers. Factors which may influence effectiveness may occur at various levels, including the: patient, intervention, therapist, and service.

Factors which have been considered to influence psychotherapy effectiveness in other meta-analyses/reviews include: (1) treatment dosage, (2) type of therapy, (3) presenting problem/diagnosis, (4) sample inclusion of clients who end prematurely (i.e. intention-to-treat[ITT]). Other factors which have received less meta-analytic attention include that which relate to the type of service providing treatment. With that, we considered: (5) setting (i.e. outpatient vs. residential), (6) sector (e.g. primary care, University, secondary care), and then geographically, the (7) country and (8) continent. We also considered the (9) stage of the hour-glass model (1 or 3) as a moderator of interest, and (10) whether the study was conducted retrospectively (e.g. audit) or prospectively. All of these variables provide means to categorical distinguish studies and analyse differences between. Continuous variables which we considered to be of interest in predicting psychotherapy outcomes (i.e. meta-regressive) included: (1) year of publication, (2) rate of demographic characteristics, (3) methodological rating, and (4) before treatment sample severity.

0.1 Aims & Hypotheses

The aim of this study was to review the effectiveness of psychological treatments conducted in routine treatment settings. This will include a systematic search and meta-analysis of all eligible articles published to the present day (April 2020).
