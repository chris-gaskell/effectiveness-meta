The Effectiveness of Psychotherapy Outcomes in Routine Settings

Primary Meta-Analysis

0.12 Samples

Studies differed in the type of self-report outcome variable employed (anxiety, depression etc.). We therefore chose to group outcome variables into three categories: (1) depression, (2) anxiety or (3) general (other). Each sample could contribute upto one outcome variable for each outcome measure group. There was subsequently different amounts of samples contributing towards each meta-analysis. Of the samples included in the current meta-analyses there was 142 for depression, 85 for anxiety, and 189 for general.

0.13 Outcome Measures

Within the three outcome measure groups, there was a wide variety of specific outcome measures used. In the depression meta-analysis (k = 142) there was XXX different self-report depression measures used. The most frequently used measure of depression was the Becks Depression Inventory (BDI I or II, k = 80), followed by the Patient Health Questionnaire (PHQ-9, k = 30) and then the Brief Symptom Inventory ([BSI] Depression Index, k = 8).

In the Anxiety meta-analysis (k = 85) there was XXX different self-report Anxiety measures used. The most frequently used measure of Anxiety was the Generalised Anxiety Disorder (GAD-7, k = 19), followed by the Becks Anxiety Inventory (BAI, k = 20) and then the Brief Symptom Inventory ([BSI] Anxiety Index, k = 8).

In the General meta-analysis (k = 189) there was XXX different self-report measures used. The most frequently used measure was the CORE (10/OM, k = 39), followed by the Brief Symptom Inventory ([BSI] Global Severity Index, k = 26), the Symptom Checklist 90 ([SCL] Global Severity Index, k = 21) and then the Outcome Questionnaire (OQ 30/45/45.2, k = 18)

0.15 Depression

The primary meta-analysis for depression included 140 samples, with a combined N of 68,193. The pooled effect-size for depression was large (d = 0.98, CI [0.9-1.06], p< 0.001). There was evidence of high heterogeneity across the included studies (I2 = 98.4%, Q[df = 139] = 3,037.46, p< 0.001).

The funnel plot for the included studies is shown in the figure below. In assessing publication bias, the funnell rank correlation test was not significant(\(\tau\) = 0.028, p = 0.629). The funnel regression test was also not significant(Z = 2.135, p = 0.035). The fail-safe N analysis (number of non-significant studies likely needed to be published to overturn the findings to a small clinically non-significant effect using the Rosenthal Approach) was 736,945
