Chapter 3 Narrative Synthesis

All of the 249 studies included in the data extraction phase were included in the narrative synthesis. Of these studies, 223 were eligible for inclusion in the meta-analysis. As a number of studies provided multiple samples the total number of samples included in the meta-analysis was 264. For the quantitative synthesis there was 249 studies and 304 samples.

3.2.1 Year of Publication

Of the 249 studies (not samples) included in the narrative synthesis, the year of publication ranged from 1984 to 2020 The median publication date was 2013. There was 299 articles published since 2000, 217 since 2010 and 128 since 2015.

3.2.2 Intention-to-treat

Studies varied by the type of sample that they analysed (i.e. completers vs. intention-to-treat [ITT]). In situations when both completers and ITT samples were reported we extracted only the ITT sample. The number of ITT samples, including those that used modified ITT or where ITT could only be ‘assumed’ was 169. The number of studies that used our more rigorous definition of ITT was 64, while the number of studies that used a completer sample was 114.

3.2.3 Sector

We recorded the sector/s that each sample represented. Because of the large number of service types, grouping classifications were employed. Outpatient samples coming from primary care services, health focused services, counselling services and voluntary services was grouped into a ‘moderate need’ group. Outpatient samples coming from secondary care or tertiary care services, specialist therapy services, behavioural health organisations or intensive out-patient programs was grouped into a ‘complex need’ group. Samples that came from University training clinics and centres were grouped together, and then residential based samples were grouped together (inpatient, day hospital, partial hospital setting). 3.2.8 Treatment Dosage

In measuring the dosage of treatment, the majority of samples either used a metric of sessions (or occasionally ‘hours’) while a smaller proportion of samples (usually inpatient) used alternative time metrics (i.e. days, weeks, months, years).

Dosage metrics were reported for the majority of study samples (k = 252). The most common metric was sessions (or hours, k = 221), followed by months (k = 12), and then days (k = 8). There was no dosage metric available for 42 samples. We converted weeks/months/years into days to allow for a more consistent metric, leaving us with two possible metrics, sessions OR time. If studies reported sample dosage but without an alternative average to ‘mean’ (i.e. median) then we converted to mean average.

TO COMPLETE TREATMENT DOSAGE. ### Trainee Clinicians

We recorded if unqualified clinicians were interventionists in each study sample. We define unqualified clinicians as staff training towards a psychology training course. Staff who are not psychologists or qualified therapists, but who have a core profession (e.g. nurses, social workers) are not recognised as trainee clinicians here. At least one trainee clinician was reported to be an interventionist in 99 samples.
