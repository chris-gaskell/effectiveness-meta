A Meta-Analysis of Psychotherapy Studies Conducted in Naturalistic Settings

Chapter 5 Additional Studies

Methods for identifying additional studies missed by the electronic database search were employed. First the reference lists of all studies that passed the full-text screening were scanned, titles that appeared relevent to the current review were identified. Second we used GoogleScholar forward citation search to scan all the papers that had cited the articles which passed the full-text screening process. Third a grey literature search using Google Scholar was conducted by combining three specific search terms (“psychotherapy”, “naturalistic”, “effectiveness”) and reviewing the first 50 pages of results. Finally, the corresponding authors of the articles identified in the full-text screening process were sent a standardised e-mail to ask for any additional relvent papers that the authors knew of. Using this approach we found 197 papers from reference lists and forward citations searching and 99 papers from grey literature/author requests. 5.1 Extraction

There was 233 studies at the data extraction stage. Our unified effect-size for meta-analysis was repeated samples Cohen’s d, dividing the mean groups differance by the PreSD and adjusting the sampling variance using the pre-post Pearon’s r. This approach is consistent with Minami et al (2008). As very few studies used this precise effect-size variant we calculated them ourself using the data reported in study manuscripts. Unfortunately a number of studies did not report sufficicent information for us to do this.

The majority of studies reported all required information apart from r. In these situations (185 studies) we e-mailed authors to request this information. Information was subsequently provided for XXX studies. For studies that remained without r at this point we imputed r using an established value (0.6, reference).

For 35 studies there was not enough information to manually calculate Cohen’s d (i.e. did not report pre-post M, SD). If the manuscript reported a variant of Cohen’s d (but not our preferred) then we used took this value. If this was not reported then we contacted authors to request the missing information. For studies that the author did not respond then we searched for alternative reported data (e.g. median, range, standard error, ANOVA, regression) and converted them to the required form (M and SD) using established estimation formulas. For situations where there remained insufficient data with which to calculate Cohen’s d then the study was not included in the meta-analysis but rather was kept in the narrative synthesis.

Of the 233 studies extracted there was 210 studies that could be included in the meta-analysis. As a number of studies provided multiple samples the total number of samples was 236. For the quantitative synthesis there was 233 studies and 261 samples.
