A Meta-Analysis of Psychotherapy Studies Conducted in Naturalistic Settings

Chapter 3 The Case for Moderators

Since the Cahill (2010) paper, there has been exponential growth in practice-based evidence. Reasons for this are likely to include: (1) shift towards a scientific-practitioner philosophy within the psychological therapies; (2) a greater number of empirically support routine outcome measures for clinicians to use; and (3) changes in health-care provision (e.g. IAPT in the UK) to systems which mandate routine outcome monitoring. A review of the literature is indicated and which takes into account current perspectives of routine practice. Unlike the Cahill (2010) review, this study is only concerned with individual psychotherapy, and therefore will not include studies with patients who access group psychotherapy only.

The aim of this study was to review the effectiveness of psychological treatments conducted in routine treatment settings. This will include a systematic search and meta-analysis of all eligible articles published to the present day (April 2020). Pre-identified factors which may moderate the effectiveness of psychotherapy under routine conditions will be explored. These factors will include care sector (primary care, secondary care, inpatient, University), country, psychological treatment (e.g. CBT, CAT) patient characteristics (diagnosis, demographics etc.) and baseline distress levels. In doing this, the study will extract (if reported) effect-sizes for measures of treatment effectiveness.
